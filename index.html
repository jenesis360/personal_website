About me

I am a doctoral student at the University of Oxford studying the most fundamental questions of particle physics. Currently, I am based at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland.

As a member of the ATLAS Experiment, I collaborate with scientists to pursue evidence for new particles produced at the Large Hadron Collider. Supervised by Alan Barr and John March-Russell, much of my research sits at the exciting interface of theory and experiment, understanding how to discover supersymmetry and dark matter.

Originally from London, England, I subsequently went to the University of Oxford, where I graduated in 2014 with a BA in Physics. I then spent a year in Canada at the Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics, supervised by Natalia Toro (now at SLAC, Stanford University), and received my MSc from the University of Waterloo.

I enjoy doing science outreach, travelling, classical music and graphic design. As an undergraduate, I designed websites and played the violin for numerous music ensembles. I have grapheme-colour synaesthesia, where my brain associates colours to letters and numbers.

Research

Particle physics

What are the fundamental building blocks of the universe? How do they interact? The Standard Model and General Relativity are our current best theories of nature that sit on the core principles of spacetime symmetries and quantum theory. Yet, due to both theoretical arguments and observational inconsistencies, we know they cannot be the whole story. We need new data from experimental tests to extend our understanding of nature.

Supersymmetry

Why is gravity so weak compared to the other forces? Why is the Higgs boson so unexpectedly light? Supersymmetry is a speculative theory that could help answer these questions. It predicts new particles which could be discovered at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN, Switzerland. More technically, I think about how to interpret LHC searches and analyse data for colourless superparticles that decay into low energy final states.

ATLAS Experiment

The ATLAS Experiment is essentially a glorified camera, capturing images of proton-proton collisions at CERN with exquisite precision. Thousands of scientists work together to ensure the detector works seamlessly to analyse data at the energy frontier. These could glimpse signatures of new particles created at high energy collisions. Specifically, I am involved with the silicon tracker subdetector.

Dark sectors

Arguably the most startling realisation of fundamental science in recent decades is that our universe is mostly dark. There is now overwhelming evidence for dark matter. It makes up over 80% of the matter in the universe, but does not emit, reflect nor absorb light. What particles is it made of? How can we understand this dark sector?

The LHC interpretation challenge

How do we interpret the results of searches pursued by LHC experiments?

Complementarity of recent 13 TeV supersymmetry searches and dark matter interplay in the pMSSM

A. Barr and Jesse Liu
Aug 18, 2016
arXiv preprint
Work presented at Manchester, CERN, Durham, Cambridge, Oxford
Slides from Oxford seminar [22 MB]

We reveal the distinct regions of parameter space where each of the six early 13 TeV ATLAS searches is most sensitive to within the same theoretical framework. Such an interpretation is difficult when just looking at simplified model limits. We also interpret searches for colourful superparticles in dark matter contexts and show they can illuminate the rich dark sector beyond classic monojet interpretations.
