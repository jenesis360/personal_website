About me

I am a doctoral student of Oxford University studying the most fundamental questions of particle physics. Currently I am based at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland.

As a member of the ATLAS experiment, I collaborate with scientists to pursue experimental evidence for new physics beyond the Standard Model at the energy frontier. Much of my research sits at the exciting interface of theory and experiment, understanding the signatures to discover supersymmetry and dark matter at the Large Hadron Collider.

I was born and grew up in London, England. For my undergraduate, I went to the University of Oxford where I graduated in 2014 with a BA in Physics. Afterwards, I spent a year in Canada at the Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics, receiving my MSc in Physics from the University of Waterloo.

I enjoy doing science outreach, travelling, classical music and am intrigued by graphic design. I have grapheme-colour synaethesia, where my brain associates colours to letters and numbers.

Research

Particle physics

What are the fundamental building blocks of the universe? How do they interact? These are the questions particle physicists ask every day. The Standard Model and General Relativity are our current best theories of nature, but from both theoretical and observational arguments, we know it cannot be the whole story.

Supersymmetry and dark matter

There are new laws of physics awaiting discovery, but what could they be? The Large Hadron Collider at CERN, Switzerland collides protons at the world's highest energies. It could create new supersymmetric particles that may help explain the mysterious dark matter in our universe.

ATLAS experiment

The ATLAS experiment is essentially a glorified camera, capturing images of proton-proton collisions with exquisite precision. Thousands of scientists work together to ensure the detector works seamlessly to analyse data at the energy frontier.

Continuous spin particles

So-called continuous spin particles arise naturally from symmetries of nature, and could deepen our understanding of gravity and electromagnetism. But are they theoretically consistent and could we one day detect them?
